# 5 Tractor Maintenance Tips That Save Nizamabad Farmers ₹20,000/Year

A tractor breakdown during harvest is not just a repair bill. It's lost work days, delayed threshing, and crops left standing in the field. Emergency repairs on a New Holland tractor typically run between ₹5,000 and ₹30,000 per incident. Add two or three breakdowns a year, plus the cost of missed sowing windows, and poorly maintained tractors easily cost Nizamabad farmers ₹20,000 or more annually.

The good news: most of that money stays in your pocket with 10 minutes of daily checks. The five tips below are exactly what our service team at Prakash Motors recommends to every New Holland owner in Nizamabad, Bodhan, Banswada, Armoor, and the surrounding taluks.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - Daily oil and coolant checks take under 5 minutes and prevent the two most common engine failures.
> - Telangana's black cotton soil clogs air filters faster than typical farm soil - clean yours every 50 hours, not 100.
> - Correct tyre pressure changes between field use (14-16 PSI rear) and road use (18-20 PSI rear).
> - Annual professional service costs ₹3,000-₹5,000; a single major breakdown costs ₹20,000-₹50,000.
> - Grease all nipple points before every paddy transplanting season - wet soil doubles wear on linkage bushings.

## Tip 1: Why Daily Oil Checks Are Non-Negotiable in Telangana

[PERSONAL EXPERIENCE] Our service team sees more engine failures from neglected oil than from any other cause. Diesel engines running in the fine dust of Nizamabad's black cotton soil degrade oil two to three times faster than engines working in loamy or sandy conditions. Dirty or low oil means metal-on-metal contact inside the engine - and that gets expensive fast.

**The right oil grade:** For most New Holland tractor models (3600, 4710, 5500 series), use SAE 15W-40 diesel engine oil that meets the CF-4 or CG-4 specification. This grade handles both Telangana's cold winter mornings (when temperatures drop to 12-15°C) and the scorching pre-monsoon heat above 42°C.

**How to check correctly:** Always check the dipstick when the engine is cold - either first thing in the morning or at least 30 minutes after shutting down. Pull the dipstick out, wipe it clean, reinsert fully, then pull again. The oil level should sit between the two marks. Top up if it falls below the lower mark.

**Warning signs you cannot ignore:** Blue or grey exhaust smoke means oil is burning inside the engine. A knocking sound under load means oil pressure is low. Either sign means stop the tractor immediately and check before you lose the engine.

**Change interval:** Change engine oil every 250 hours of operation or once a year, whichever comes first. A complete oil and filter change costs roughly ₹800-₹1,200. Replacing a seized engine costs ₹40,000+. The math is straightforward.

## Tip 2: Air Filter Cleaning Saves Your Pistons

[UNIQUE INSIGHT] Nizamabad taluk and the surrounding cotton-growing belt sits on some of the finest black cotton soil in Telangana. That fine, powdery dust - locally called "reg" soil - is exactly the particle size that passes through a worn or loosely seated air filter element and straight into your combustion chamber. The result: scored cylinder walls and cracked pistons, a repair that starts at ₹15,000.

**Paper element vs. oil bath filter:** Older New Holland models (3000 series) often use an oil bath air cleaner. Newer models (4710, 3630) use a paper dry element. Both need attention, but they're cleaned differently.

- **Paper element:** Tap it gently against a flat surface to knock loose dust free. Never wash a paper element with water - water collapses the paper fibres and ruins it. Hold it up to sunlight: if you can see pinhole tears or cannot see light through it at all, replace it.
- **Oil bath element:** Drain the old oil, wash the bowl with diesel, let it dry, then refill with fresh engine oil to the marked level.

**Cleaning schedule for Telangana:** Clean every 50 hours during land preparation and kharif sowing (June-July), when dust is heaviest. During paddy harvesting, when chaff clogs filters fast, check every 25-30 hours. Replace the paper element fully every 500 hours.

## Tip 3: Coolant Level and Overheating Prevention

Overheating is the second biggest engine killer after oil neglect. A blown head gasket - the most common overheating result - costs ₹8,000 to ₹15,000 to replace, not counting labour and downtime during the kharif season when your tractor is needed most.

**How to top up correctly:** Use a 1:1 mix of clean distilled water and standard radiator coolant (any brand with corrosion inhibitor works). Do not use bore water or tap water - the minerals cause scale buildup inside the radiator passages over time. Top up only into the overflow reservoir, never directly into the radiator when the engine is warm.

**Critical safety rule:** Never open the radiator cap when the engine is hot. Pressurised coolant will spray out and cause serious burns. Wait at least 30 minutes after shutdown.

**Signs of a coolant leak:** White steam from the exhaust (coolant burning in the combustion chamber), a sweet smell near the engine, or white mineral deposits around hose connections. Any of these signs means bring the tractor in for inspection before the next working day.

**Maintenance interval:** Flush and replace coolant every two years. This removes accumulated rust and scale that reduces cooling efficiency. Cost: under ₹500 in coolant plus your time.

## Tip 4: Getting Tyre Pressure Right for Telangana Fields

Tyre pressure is not one-size-fits-all. The correct pressure depends on where your tractor is working. This is one of the most overlooked adjustments among farmers in the Bodhan and Banswada areas, where fields and rocky approach roads are both part of the daily route.

**Field use (paddy transplanting, wet soil):** Lower the rear tyre pressure to 14-16 PSI. Lower pressure spreads the contact patch across a wider area, giving better traction in wet, loose soil. Running high pressure in waterlogged paddy fields causes the tyres to dig in and the tractor to bog down.

**Road and transport use:** Increase rear tyre pressure to 18-20 PSI before driving on paved roads or gravel tracks. High-speed road use at low pressure generates heat inside the tyre and damages the sidewalls from the inside out.

**Rocky terrain caution:** The rocky track approaches near Bodhan and Banswada are hard on tyres. Over-inflated tyres (above 22 PSI) on rocky ground transfer all impact to the rim, causing rim bends and even cracks. Keep pressure in the 18-20 PSI range on mixed terrain.

**Checking frequency:** Check tyre pressure at least once a month with a proper gauge. Eyeballing a large tractor tyre is not reliable - a tyre can be 6 PSI low and still look fine to the eye. Cost of ignoring: uneven wear across the tread means replacing tyres 2-3 years early, at ₹4,000-₹6,000 per tyre.

## Tip 5: Greasing the Right Points at the Right Time

[PERSONAL EXPERIENCE] Grease is cheap. Worn kingpins, seized lift links, and damaged PTO bearings are not. A complete front axle rebuild costs upwards of ₹12,000 - and it's almost always preventable with regular greasing.

**Key grease points on New Holland tractors:**

- Front axle king pins (both sides)
- Tie rod ends (both sides)
- Lift arm pins and 3-point linkage bushings
- Top link pin
- Lower link draft pins
- PTO shaft splines and bearing housing
- Steering cylinder pins (on power steering models)

Use only moly-based (molybdenum disulphide) grease, not general-purpose lithium grease. Moly grease handles the high-pressure, slow-movement conditions found in kingpins and linkage bushings far better than standard grease.

**Frequency during paddy season:** Grease all points before paddy transplanting begins and again midway through the season. Constant submersion in wet, muddy water washes grease out of exposed joints far faster than normal dry-field work. Skipping this step during the transplanting season is how most kingpin failures happen.

**General schedule:** Grease all points every 50 hours during normal operation. Before storing the tractor at the end of the season, grease every point to prevent corrosion during the off period.

## Bonus Tip: Battery Maintenance in Telangana Heat

Telangana's summers push ambient temperatures above 42°C. That kind of heat accelerates water evaporation from conventional lead-acid batteries. A dry battery cell damages itself permanently within a few charge cycles.

**Monthly check:** Open the battery caps and check electrolyte levels. Top up with distilled water only - never tap water or bore water. The electrolyte should cover the plates by about 10-12 mm. Do not overfill.

**Terminal cleaning:** White or blue powdery buildup on battery terminals is corrosion. It increases electrical resistance and causes hard-starting problems, especially on cold winter mornings. Clean terminals with a wire brush, then apply a thin layer of petroleum jelly (Vaseline) to prevent future corrosion.

**Short-run days:** Avoid running the tractor for less than 20-30 minutes at a time without real load. Short idle runs discharge the battery faster than the alternator can recharge it. If you need to run the tractor briefly, let it run at medium throttle for at least 20 minutes afterwards to bring the battery back up.

## When to Bring Your Tractor to a Service Centre

Some work is worth doing yourself. Other work needs trained hands and the right equipment. Bring your New Holland to Prakash Motors service centre in Nizamabad for:

- **Annual service:** Full inspection, oil change, filter replacement, belt check, and adjustment. Cost: ₹3,000-₹5,000 depending on model.
- **Hydraulic oil change:** Every 1,000 hours. Hydraulic oil that breaks down causes spool valve wear and loss of implement lift - a ₹10,000+ repair.
- **Belt tension and replacement:** Fan belt, alternator belt, and power steering pump belt tension should be checked by a technician annually. A snapped belt in the middle of harvest leaves you stranded.
- **Major overhaul signs:** Persistent blue smoke, power loss under load, abnormal engine noise, or hydraulic pressure drop are all signs that internal work is needed. Catching these early means the repair is far cheaper than waiting until the engine seizes.
